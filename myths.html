Debunking Anorexia Myths

  • MYTH: Anorexia is just about wanting to be thin.
    • TRUTH: Anorexia is not soley about appearance of weight. It's a complex mental health disorder often rooted in a need for control, perfectionism, trauma, or low self-esteem. It involves emotional and psychological struggles, not just physical ones.
  • MYTH: People with anorexia just need to "eat more"
    • TRUTH: Recovery involves adressing the underlying emotional, psychological, and behavioral issues throught therapy, medical treatment, and support systems. Simply eating more doesn't resolve the mental health aspects of anorexia.
  • MYTH: Only teenage girls struggle with anorexia.
    • TRUTH: Anorexia affects people of all ages, genders, races, and socioeconomic backgrounds. While it is more commonly diagnosed in women, men and nonbinary people can also struggle with anorexia. Older adults and children are not immune to anorexia either.
  • MYTH: Once someone gains weight, they're "cured"
    • TRUTH: Weight restoration is a step of recovery, but it does not address the psychologial components of anorexia. Long-term recovery involves ongoing therapy and support to address distorted thoughts and prevent relapse.
  • MYTH: Anorexia is just a phase or attention-seeking.
    • TRUTH: Anorexia is a not a choice or a cry for attention; it's a serious and potentionally life-threatening mental illness. Many individuals suffer in silence - trying to hide their struggles. Many feel an immense shame about their behaviors.
  • MYTH: If someone eats, they can't have anoreixa.
    • TRUTH: People with anorexia may eat small amounts, restrict certain food groups, or eat in a controlled manner. It's the extreme fear of weight gain and obsessive thoughts about food that define the disorder, not the complete absence of eating.
  • MYTH: You can tell if someone has anorexia by looking at them / all people with anorexia are underweight.
    • TRUTH: Not all individuals with anorexia appear visibily ill or underweight. The mental health illness is about behaviors and thought patterns, which are not always reflected in someone's appearance.
